Strategic Challenge:
Closing the Aspiration-Acquisition Gap

"Consumers aspire to Dior but purchase competitors—the gap lies in perceived value accessibility, not brand desirability."

Primary research revealed a critical disconnect: while luxury consumers ranked Dior, Hermès, and Chanel as most desirable brands, actual ownership skewed toward Coach, Louis Vuitton, and Chanel. The strategic imperative was clear—expand e-commerce into US and Asia markets, target millennials earning $60K+, and diversify price points through material innovation.

Consumer Purchase Behavior

Most consumers own 3+ luxury handbags, search for new options at least once annually, and spend primarily under $1,000—though a significant segment invests $1,000-$5,000 per purchase. Primary purchase drivers: Style/Design (1st), Quality (2nd), Investment Value (3rd).

Brand Perception Gap

Desired brands: Dior, Hermès, Kate Spade, Chloé, Louis Vuitton, Chanel. Actual ownership: Coach, Chanel, Louis Vuitton. Dior's perceived "expensive" positioning without visible pricing creates friction in the consideration phase.

Channel Accessibility Issue

No e-commerce functionality outside EU for leather goods. Consumers unable to find Dior in secondary retailers they prefer. Lower handbag category awareness compared to RTW and beauty divisions.

  • Design diversity across 8 handbag models
  • Italian leather craftsmanship heritage

Weaknesses

  • No visible pricing on website creates "expensive" perception
  • Second Creative Director change in 5 years
  • No e-commerce outside EU for leather goods
  • Lower handbag recognition vs. RTW and beauty
  • Limited secondary retailer presence
  • Weaker social media presence vs. competitors

Opportunities

  • Promote Small Leather Goods (SLG) as entry point
  • Launch North America/Asia e-commerce
  • Partner with Net-a-Porter and secondary retailers
  • Pre-owned trade-in program (Green initiative)
  • Mobile app development
  • Alternative materials (canvas, nylon) for accessible pricing

Threats

  • Chanel e-commerce launch with competitive positioning
  • Growing rental market (Bag Borrow or Steal)
  • Competitors' active social media (Gucci, Hermès)
  • Fast fashion accessibility eroding luxury appeal
  • Economic uncertainty in key luxury markets
  • Lower social media mention ranking vs. peers
Segmentation & Targeting

Brand Positioning & Target Consumer

Target Consumer Profile

Demographics

Fashion-forward women, 25-40 years old, single or married, college-educated, household income $60,000+/year, residing in major US and Asian metropolitan areas.

Psychographics

Digital natives with high social engagement. Status-motivated consumers who view handbags as investment pieces. Subscribe to W, Vogue, and WSJ via mobile/tablet.

Behavioral Patterns

Cross-shops between luxury brands, influenced by celebrity and blogger endorsements, conducts thorough research before purchase, willing to wait for the right piece.

Media Consumption

Instagram, Facebook, Snapchat active users. Fashion blog followers. Mobile-first content consumption. Responds to digital advertising and influencer recommendations.

Strategic Insights

Key Learnings

On Luxury E-commerce

Digital Reluctance as Strategic Liability

Digital hesitation in luxury is no longer brand protection—it's competitive liability. Competitors who embraced e-commerce early captured younger consumers who will define the market for decades. The question isn't whether to digitize, but how to maintain brand equity while doing so.

On Price Transparency

Information Access as Brand Expectation

Hidden pricing doesn't create exclusivity—it creates friction. Modern luxury consumers expect information access; mystique should come from product quality and experience, not opacity. Transparency builds trust without diminishing desirability.

On Material Innovation

Accessibility Without Dilution

Alternative materials (canvas, nylon) can create entry points without brand dilution when executed with design integrity. The key is maintaining craftsmanship standards while expanding price accessibility—democratizing access, not quality.

On Consumer Journey

Aspiration-Acquisition Bridge

The gap between brand aspiration and actual purchase reveals strategic opportunity. When consumers desire a brand but buy competitors, the barrier is rarely desirability—it's accessibility, whether through price, channel, or information availability.
